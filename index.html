Introduction

When approaching this project, I knew exactly which archive to evaluate. The Davie County Register of Deeds was vital to the research conducted for my History Capstone. The Register of Deeds archival database houses scanned deeds, mortgages, leases, and other contracts specific to land/ property in Davie County, North Carolina, separated by categories including Full System (Indexing and Imaging Combined Retrieval), Imaging System Only, and Scanned Index Books. The categories are also divided by date, ranging from 1837 to the present. Using this archive, I cross-referenced it with the U.S. Census Bureau and North Carolina Department of Education records to confirm the identities of African American landowners and to identify the factors that shaped the lives of those who did and did not own land during the Jim Crow era.

Mission Statement

There is a ‘Welcome Message’ that reads like a mission statement:

“All of us here at the Register of Deeds office remain committed to protecting and preserving Davie County’s records in order to secure their integrity for all time. We remain ready and willing to help all citizens and will continue to be knowledgeable in our field, courteous to our patrons, and efficient in our work to bring you remarkable customer service.”

Background

The Davie County Register of Deeds is not an open-source, web-based application. Instead, the database is privately hosted by Logan Systems Inc. in Greensboro, North Carolina, and provides equitable access to official public records. The state government hosts the leading information page, which serves as the entry point to this and other databases. On the leading page, the state provides links to various databases under the jurisdiction of the Register of Deeds, including vital records, marriage licenses, notary public, and others.

To enter the separately hosted database, click the “Search Online Records” option. Upon entering the database, the introductory page offers another welcome message, stating that Davie County has updated the website to improve efficiency for county residents and all site users. There is an important message that, when clicked, is dated 2023. The last option before opening the database is a legal disclaimer stating that the documents on the site are unofficial records and that, if users need official documents, they must visit the Davie County Register of Deeds office in North Carolina. Clicking the blue-highlighted acknowledgment opens the next page, taking the user to the database.

Objective of Investigation

For this project, the fictional client is the Davie County Register of Deeds office. The office has recently been inundated with inquiries and complaints about user experiences using the database. They have reached out for an independent system analysis to identify issues affecting user experience and provide actionable suggestions to improve the system. The independent report will be forwarded to the site host, Logan Systems Inc., for proposed updates. To achieve this goal, Nielsen’s Heuristics are used to identify issues affecting user experience, usability tests are conducted with a panel of five participants, and the patron complaints and inquiries are examined and organized to address specified user engagement difficulties.

Evaluation of the Current Database

The most significant system weaknesses in comparison to Montgomery County, Maryland’s Digital Repository for Land Records include the following Nielsen’s Heuristics.

Nielsen’s Heuristics

  • 1. Visibility of System Status – Communicate to users the current status of the system. Keeping users informed builds trust in the system, fostering repeat use.
  • 3. User Control and Freedom – Supporting the ‘undo’ and ‘redo’ site functions gives users a quick exit in case of mistakes made during use.
  • 4. Consistency and Standards – Follow platform and industry conventions.
  • 7. Flexibility and Efficiency of Use – Create ‘shortcuts’ for seasoned users.
  • 10. Help and Documentation – Help and Documentation should be displayed and easily accessed.

Solutions

Visibility of System Status – On the North Carolina government’s homepage, a sitemap is available to help users navigate the system.

User Control and Freedom – Although the ‘Back’ function is supported, a more visible “Home” button would help users.

Consistency and Standards – The banner menu should be visible.

Flexibility and Efficiency of Use – Users can optimize the site for personal use.

Help and Documentation – There is no ‘Help’ option anywhere on the database.

Usability Test Results

Participants were asked to search and retrieve specific documents from the website. They were given 20 minutes to locate two documents.

  • Please locate the deed to the property owned by Abner Steel (1883).
  • Locate the canceled debt and deed for Henry Patterson (1908).
  • Search parameters include name and date of deed.

The usability test revealed it was very difficult for novice users to navigate the database.

Patron Inquiries & Complaints

Patron complaints include 40% requesting a ‘Help’ or ‘Contact Us’ option added to the database, while 45% requested guidance and instructions.

Another 15% of complaints relate to search results clarity.
